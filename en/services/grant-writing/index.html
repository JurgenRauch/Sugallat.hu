Grant writing and project preparation

When is it worth seeking expert assistance for grant writing?

Engaging an expert becomes truly important when the stakes of the grant application are already significant from a business perspective, and a rejection could result in serious time or financial losses. In such cases, the accurate interpretation of grant requirements, verification of eligibility, as well as the professional and financial grounding of the project are essential for a successful submission.

Grant application structure and key content elements

A grant application essentially consists of several interconnected sections that together form the professional and financial foundation of the project. These include the project description, the definition of objectives and indicators, the implementation timeline, the detailed budget, as well as the attachment of required annexes and declarations. Assembling these in a unified, logical structure is a fundamental prerequisite for a successful evaluation.

Key information about grants for businesses and non-profit organisations

When submitting grant applications without expert help, there is a significant risk of formal errors, misinterpretation of eligibility requirements, and inadequate content development. If a proposal is submitted with formal errors, it can easily be rejected. A company should pay special attention to formal details before submission. During the application process, especially for first-time applicants, misunderstandings about the requirements can quickly lead to mistakes. Grant writing errors often fail not on the professional content, but on small formal details.

A grant application typically includes a detailed professional description of the project, the definition of objectives and indicators, an implementation timeline, a budget, as well as mandatory annexes and declarations. For an organisation, verifying eligibility is particularly important.

Most grant applications fail not because the idea is poor, but because the implementation plan is not convincing. Rejection can result from missing annexes, incorrect cost calculations, unrealistic commitments, or the project not aligning precisely with the objectives of the call for proposals.
